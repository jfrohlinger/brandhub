The Industry Problem

Small Brands. Big Promises.
Short Runways.

Every distributor knows the story. A passionate founder, a promising liquid, an ambitious pitch — and six months later, dead inventory and an unanswered phone.

Small and emerging brands are the lifeblood of portfolio differentiation. Distributors like Southern Glazer's take them on with the best intentions — diversifying the book, supporting the craft movement, answering supplier demand.

But the reality is that most small suppliers are chronically undercapitalized. They need distributor purchases to make payroll. They need the credibility of distribution to raise their next round. They're playing a short game while asking distributors to make a long-term commitment.

The result? Distributors bear 100% of the operational burden while the brand extracts the capital benefit — then struggles to execute the market support that justifies the listing.

📦
Dead Inventory
Distributor capital is locked in goods that stop moving when brand support evaporates. Warehouse space and carrying costs compound the damage over months and quarters.
🧠
Diluted Sales Focus
Every small brand on a rep's book competes for mindshare against established revenue drivers. Without programming and tools, reps default to the path of least resistance.
⚙️
Back-Office Burden
Pricing, programming, and depletion inputs don't care how small a brand is. Small brands don't justify dedicated resources — but they consume them anyway.
🔀
No Standardization
Every brand operates on its own terms — different buy windows, different POS items, different processes, different timelines. Southern's teams constantly adapt to each brand's way of working instead of the other way around. The result is operational fragmentation at scale.
